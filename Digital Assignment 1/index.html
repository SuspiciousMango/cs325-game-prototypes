Bored in the Office

  • Originality: The main focus of originality in my game is the sound design. I recorded all the voicelines myself to add personality and originality to an otherwise pretty basic game.
  • Technical Merit: To get around having to manually position each sprite on screen, I created them all as layers on a GIMP canvas and exported them separately. They are all tecnhically the size of the entire screen, and to counteract this the "buttons" that activate each sound and animation are really just the program checking for whether or not the pointer is within a certain square. Is this the best solution? Not necessarily, but it was an interesting workaround I came up with after realizing doing the normal phaser sprite detection wouldn't work on my game window-sized sprites.
  • Prototype Postmortem: I learned that it is both way easier and way harder to prototype a basic game in a week than I thought. Phaser is clearly very powerful, and I was able to do a lot really easily that I wouldn't have been able to do with other engines. On the flip side, when something went wrong, it tended to go very wrong. For instance: the hardest part of making this prototype was just learning the phaser syntax and how certain functions worked. It took me a solid 3 hours to get my pointer listener working (and to be honest I'm not entirely sure why what I did ended up making it work opposed to everything else I tried) and the rest of my code was reliant on that, so it was impossible for me to pivot to implementing different features during that time. Additionally I cannot seem to get console.log to appear anywhere when running from the local python server, so debugging is basically just changing the lines of code that I think might be the problem until something works. A very inefficient system that, along with the pointer listener not working, severely limited the time I had to actually implement features.

  • Prototype Closest Other Game: Any idle clicker game.
  • High Concept: Bored in the Office is about a man who is, well, bored in the office. Click on the world around him to try to entertain him. Discover what interactions are possible.
  • Theme: Instead of choosing a theme, I decided to choose a challenge: "you cannot win with sound off." In my opinion, the "win condition" of Bored at the Office is the player having an enjoyable reaction to the effects, and although I guess technically possible, it would be very hard to have an enjoyable reaction to the game if you could not hear the voice lines I put so much effort into.
  • Mandated Variety: This game uses clickable buttons (although they are hidden from view) and no randomness. It is designed towards the explorer play style and could best be described as a static adventure game.
  • Prototype Goal: What game mechanic is this prototype evaluating?
  • Player Experience Goals: The goal for the player is simply to experience the "world" created by the one scene. There should be a sense of satisfaction when they discover a new secret action and the ridiculous voice acting is designed to make the entire game humorous.
  • Gameplay: The game has a very simple instruction set. The player simply clicks on the scene around them, and watches what reaction happens. Certain places on the scene are invisible buttons that, when clicked, cause some action or interaction to occur. If this game were to continue development, the idea is that every interaction would be fully animated, but for the sake of saving time most animations have not been implemented. I would also like to focus more on interaction rather than reaction. For this demo, most of the responses are reactions, but eventually the addition of more interactive elements (akin to classic adventure games: "find this item here that can be used over here") would lead to more interesting gameplay and the ability to even have levels and transitions between them (clicking on a car in the parking lot outside may cause the scene to shift from the main character's work to his home)
  • Strategies: The only two "strategies" that really exist for this game are selectively clicking on things that seem like they might do something or just rapid fire clicking on everything and waiting for reactions.
  • Story/Setting/Premise: This game originated from the fact that I didn't have an idea for a game to make, so I decided to channel my lack of an idea into a game about being bored and not having anything to do. Since the mechanics themselves are nothing spectacular, I decided to focus on the visual and auditory elements to capture the player. The lore of the game can be summed up in the title: the main character is bored at the office. Everything else is left up to the player to interpret or ignore as they please. They can take the game as some metaphysical commentary, or just laugh at my really bad voice acting.
  • Target Audience: Anyone and everyone who enjoys clicking their mouse and having a good laugh.
  • Play Time: In theory the game could take as long as the player wishes depending on how long they want to spend searching for all the secrets. I expect that a normal game with all features fully implemented and multiple scenes to explore could take about an hour or so to find everything, although my prototype lacks most of those features and so only takes a few minutes to find everything
